# Chargeback Tracker: Business Requirements Document

## Executive Summary

This document outlines the business case for developing a specialized web application designed for e-commerce operations teams to centrally manage and dispute payment chargebacks. Currently, many direct-to-consumer (DTC) brands, particularly those in the $500K–$5M annual revenue range, struggle with disorganized, manual chargeback processes. This often leads to missed submission deadlines and lost revenue from winnable disputes.

Our proposed solution provides a single source of truth for all open chargebacks, featuring deadline countdowns and dynamic, reason-code-specific evidence checklists. By streamlining this critical, high-stakes process, we empower operations associates to efficiently manage disputes, significantly increasing chargeback win rates and recovering revenue that would otherwise be lost. Onboarding is designed to always succeed — via Stripe OAuth for the majority of merchants, and via a manual-entry fallback for those on non-standard Stripe setups.

## Business Problem

For small to medium-sized DTC e-commerce brands, managing payment chargebacks is a significant and often costly operational challenge. The typical process involves:

*   **Scattered Information:** Chargeback notifications arrive via email, appear in a payment processor dashboard (e.g., Stripe), and are often tracked manually in spreadsheets or notepads. This leads to a fragmented view of open disputes.
*   **Missed Deadlines:** Each chargeback has a strict, short deadline for evidence submission. Without a centralized tracking and alert system, operations associates frequently miss these windows, automatically forfeiting the dispute and the associated revenue.
*   **Inefficient Evidence Collection:** Gathering the correct evidence (e.g., shipping proof, customer communication, terms of service) is often manual, repetitive, and time-consuming. The specific evidence required varies significantly by chargeback reason code, leading to confusion and errors for non-expert staff.
*   **Lost Revenue:** The combined effect of missed deadlines and insufficient evidence results in a lower chargeback win rate, directly impacting profitability. For brands handling 5–30 chargebacks per month, even a small percentage increase in win rate can translate to thousands of dollars in recovered revenue monthly.
*   **Operational Burden:** The manual, error-prone nature of chargeback management consumes valuable time from operations teams, diverting resources from core business activities.

Current solutions (e.g., Stripe's dispute interface, general-purpose spreadsheets) lack the proactive tracking, centralized visibility, and tailored guidance required to effectively manage chargebacks at scale for this segment.

## Business Objectives

The primary business objectives for the Chargeback Tracker are to:

*   **Increase User Chargeback Win Rates:** Equip operations teams with the tools and guidance to effectively dispute chargebacks, thereby recovering a greater percentage of disputed revenue for our users.
*   **Reduce Operational Overhead:** Minimize the manual effort and time spent by operations associates on managing chargebacks, freeing them to focus on higher-value tasks.
*   **Improve Compliance and Timeliness:** Ensure users can consistently meet chargeback submission deadlines, eliminating losses due to administrative oversight.
*   **Establish Market Leadership:** Become the go-to, indispensable tool for chargeback management for small to mid-sized DTC e-commerce brands.
*   **Drive Recurring Revenue:** Build a subscription-based product that delivers demonstrable value, fostering long-term customer relationships and sustainable business growth.

## Target Audience

The primary target audience for the Chargeback Tracker is:

*   **Role:** Operations Associate, Customer Service Manager, or a similar role within a DTC e-commerce brand.
*   **Company Size:** Brands generating $500,000 to $5,000,000 in annual revenue.
*   **E-commerce Platform:** Primarily on Shopify.
*   **Payment Processor:** Primarily using Stripe for payment processing.
*   **Chargeback Volume:** Typically handling between 5 and 30 chargebacks per month.
*   **User Profile:** These individuals are generally not developers. They are hands-on, detail-oriented, but often overwhelmed by administrative tasks and require intuitive, easy-to-use software that can be adopted without extensive training or IT support. They are highly motivated to solve problems that directly impact the company's bottom line.

This segment is often too small for large enterprise chargeback solutions but too large for manual spreadsheet tracking to be efficient or effective. They represent a significant underserved market struggling with a high-impact, solvable problem.

### Distribution & Go-to-Market (early hypothesis)
Primary channel is content marketing to DTC ops leads (LinkedIn posts on chargeback cost analysis + a free "chargeback readiness" template) driving free-trial signups. Secondary channel is partnerships with Shopify-adjacent tools whose users overlap with our ICP (ShipStation, Gorgias). Distribution is not solved in the MVP itself — this note flags it so the post-MVP roadmap explicitly picks up a distribution owner, rather than assuming users will find us organically.

## Success Metrics

Metrics are ordered from *directly measurable in our own database on day one* to *business-level lagging indicators*. The first two are the ones we quote on the roadmap; the rest are supporting context.

### 1. North-star product metric (measurable in-app)
*   **On-time submission rate:** % of disputes whose local status is marked "Submitted" *before* the Stripe `due_by` timestamp. **MVP target: 80%.** This is the single strongest signal that the product is doing its core job. We can compute it directly from our own data — no survey required.

### 2. Supporting product metrics (also measurable in-app)
*   **Time to first action:** median hours between a dispute appearing in the dashboard and the first checklist-item check by the user.
*   **Checklist completion rate:** % of active disputes where the user has marked ≥ 80% of checklist items complete before submission.
*   **Expired-without-action count:** number of disputes that hit their deadline while local status was still `Open` or `Drafting`. This is the failure mode we exist to prevent — we want this at zero.

### 3. User engagement
*   Weekly Active Users (WAU) and session frequency per user.
*   Average disputes tracked per active user per month.
*   Retention rate of paying customers (monthly / quarterly).

### 4. Business metrics
*   Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR).
*   Customer Lifetime Value (CLTV).
*   Average Revenue Per User (ARPU).
*   Free-trial → paid conversion rate.

### 5. Qualitative (supplemental only)
*   User feedback interviews and surveys on perceived win-rate improvement and time saved. Supplements the hard metrics above — never replaces them.

## Constraints & Assumptions

### Constraints

*   **MVP Delivery Timeline:** A functional Minimum Viable Product (MVP) must be developed and ready for user testing/launch within 4 weeks.
*   **No PII/Card Data Storage:** The application will not store any sensitive personally identifiable information (PII) or credit card numbers beyond what is inherently provided by Stripe's API for chargeback identification (e.g., last 4 digits of card). All sensitive data handling remains within Stripe.
*   **Self-Serve Product:** The tool must be intuitive and easy to use without requiring developer assistance or extensive training for operations personnel. Onboarding and core functionality should be discoverable and self-explanatory.
*   **Web App Only:** The initial MVP will be a browser-based web application; no mobile application development is planned.
*   **Initial Integration Scope:** MVP focuses on integrating with Stripe for payment processing and implicitly supports Shopify users through Stripe. A manual dispute entry path is included as a first-class fallback so the product still works for merchants whose Stripe setup blocks the OAuth flow.
*   **Tech Stack:** Next.js + TypeScript on the front-end; Postgres for persistence; deployed to Vercel with a Vercel Cron trigger for the daily sync and email jobs.

### Assumptions

*   **Problem Urgency & Willingness to Pay (hypothesis, validation planned):** We assume DTC ops teams will pay $49–$99/month to prevent an average of $500–$3,000/month in lost chargeback revenue (a ~10× value ratio). **Validation:** during MVP beta we will A/B test paywall pricing at $49 and $99 and measure conversion. If conversion < 5% at either price, the pricing hypothesis is falsified and we revisit before scaling acquisition.
*   **Stripe API Sufficiency:** Stripe's API provides sufficient data points (chargeback IDs, reason codes, deadlines, dispute status, associated transaction details) to power the core functionality of tracking and generating evidence checklists.
*   **User Trust & Data Sharing:** Ops teams are willing to connect their Stripe accounts to a third-party application for enhanced chargeback management — but a meaningful minority will not or cannot, which is why manual entry is a first-class path (see Risks).
*   **Reason Code Consistency:** Chargeback reason codes and their associated evidence requirements are relatively consistent across time and sufficiently documented by Stripe/card networks to build reliable, helpful checklists.
*   **Value Proposition Clarity:** The value proposition of preventing lost revenue and saving time will resonate clearly with operations associates and their management.
*   **Market Opportunity:** The segment of $500K–$5M DTC brands using Shopify + Stripe is large enough to sustain a viable business.

## Risks

*   **Market Risk:**
    *   **Low Perceived Value / Willingness to Pay:** Target users may not view the problem as critical enough to warrant a dedicated, paid solution, opting for free, albeit inferior, alternatives (spreadsheets, Stripe dashboard alone).
    *   **Competition:** Existing solutions (larger enterprise chargeback management platforms, or enhanced features within Stripe itself) could expand down-market or erode our differentiation.
    *   **Ignorance of the Problem:** Some brands may not accurately track their chargeback losses and therefore underestimate the value of a solution.
*   **Product Risk:**
    *   **Onboarding friction / OAuth incompatibility:** A meaningful minority of DTC merchants are on Stripe Connect via Shopify Payments (or Stripe Express/Custom accounts), which complicates the standard OAuth flow. **Mitigation:** manual dispute entry is a first-class Must-have path, so onboarding is never fully blocked and every merchant can be a user on day 1. See PRD Feature 6.
    *   **Stripe API Limitations / Changes:** Unexpected limitations in Stripe's API, or frequent changes, could hinder functionality or require significant rework.
    *   **Accuracy of Checklists:** It may be challenging to create universally accurate and helpful reason-code-specific evidence checklists due to nuances in card network rules or specific bank requirements. Inaccurate advice could lead to user frustration. Mitigation: a generic-checklist wildcard so an unmapped reason code is still usable.
    *   **User Adoption / Usability:** Despite efforts, the product might not be intuitive enough for non-technical users, leading to low adoption or poor retention.
    *   **Data Security Concerns:** While we minimize PII, users may still have general security concerns about connecting a third-party app to their payment processor, impacting trust and adoption.
*   **Execution Risk:**
    *   **MVP Scope Creep:** The ambitious 4-week MVP timeline could be missed if scope is not tightly managed, delaying market entry and feedback.
    *   **Integration Complexity:** Integrating with Stripe's API, handling webhooks, and ensuring data synchronization can be more complex or time-consuming than anticipated.
    *   **Technical Debt:** Rapid development to meet the MVP deadline may introduce technical debt that hinders future feature development or scalability.
*   **Monetization Risk:**
    *   **Pricing Model Mismatch:** Difficulty in finding a pricing model that aligns with perceived value for our target audience and supports sustainable business growth. (Being validated per the hypothesis above.)
    *   **Churn:** If users don't see immediate and tangible value (e.g., increased win rates), churn rates could be high.
